TESTIMONIES



I was born and raised in Mbale, a place of both beauty and struggle. My childhood was anything but easy. Poverty wasn't just around me, it was within my home. I remember the days i wpould walk to school barefoot, not because i wanted to, but because we couldn't aafford shoes. Many nights we slept on empty stomachs, and i watched my mother cry silently, thinking we were aslleep. Hunger and pain became a language i learned early. Growing up in that environment taught me one thing; never to be blind to someone else's suffering. Life had already shown me too much, too early. But it also planted a seed in me, a longing to change my own situation right away. I left Mbale with a heavy heart and a hopeful spirit. I didn’t have much, but I carried my experiences like sacred lessons. Eventually, I found myself in places where I could breathe a little easier. I worked, struggled, saved, and slowly found stability. But even as my life improved, I couldn’t shake the memories of those I had left behind the children with swollen bellies, the mothers with tear-stained faces, and the elderly who sat by the roadside with hands outstretched. That’s when I knew; I wasn’t supposed to escape the pain just for myself. I was meant to return, not always physically, but through action. And so, I began helping the needy. I started small, distributing clothes, sharing food, paying school fees where I could. One by one, face by face, I began to see the difference love and compassion could make. Charity, for me, isn’t just about giving. It’s about healing. Each act is a quiet rebellion against the hopelessness I once felt. Each child who smiles is a reminder that no struggle is in vain. From Mbale’s dusty roads to the hands I now hold through my outreach, my story has come full circle. My past still hurts, and there are days I cry in silence, remembering where I came from. But I no longer carry shame, only purpose. If my pain can bring light to another, then I thank God for every tear I ever shed




My name is Hope, and I come from the vibrant yet often forgotten town of Mbale. My journey has been anything but smooth. I was once just a girl with dreams far bigger than my circumstances. Growing up in Mbale taught me how to survive but more importantly, it taught me how to feel deeply for others. I remember walking through the narrow streets, watching young girls drop out of school, and elderly women labor under burdens no one should bear. I felt powerless. But deep down, I promised myself that one day, I would return, not just with words, but with change. Years passed, and I got the opportunity to lead. As president of our club, I found my voice and my purpose. That position wasn’t about titles or recognition, it was a calling. Through the club, we organized drives, visited orphanages, and supported struggling families. I discovered how leadership can be a tool for healing when it's rooted in compassion. But even as I stood in front of crowds, speaking with confidence, there was always a part of me that remembered the little girl in Mbale, the one who cried herself to sleep, the one who knew hunger like a close friend. That memory never left me. It became my fuel. After my term ended, I didn’t stop. In fact, I couldn’t. I turned my efforts to full time charity work. It’s not always easy. There are days the burden feels heavy, and the needs seem endless. But when I look into the eyes of a child who now sleeps under a roof, or a widow who can feed her family because someone showed up for her, I know it's all worth it. From the dusty corners of Mbale to boardrooms and charity events, my journey has been one of transformation. I am no longer just a former club president. I am a woman who chose to turn pain into purpose, and memories into movement. If I could speak to that little girl I once was, I’d tell her; your story will matter. And to any woman out there who feels broken or invisible, know this, your past does not define your capacity to create change. Sometimes, the most powerful leaders are born in the quietest places.



About us

The Care Club Charity is a Non profit and empowerment organization located in Plot 7 Cathedral Avenue, Mbale – Uganda. Founded in 2022, it is fundamentally free from political, clans, religion and ethnic divisions. Its missions are to protect, repair, motivate, educate, care for and help build people in difficulty. We have worked continuously with the communities through projects of education, rehabilitation and skilling of youths and teen mothers through our campaign 'LETS SAVE THE FUTURE GENERATION' Under this campaign, we have seen major transformations in many who once used their bodies to fend for themselves and their families give it all up , acquiring skills , employed and are living a much better life . We have also taken many back to school.

WE BELIEVE WHERE HOPE IS REBORN, THERE IS A REASON TO LIVE, WHICH IS PURPOSE, AND IT CAN ONLY BE FOUND IN GOD.
